# AI Tells Checklist

Use this checklist to diagnose and rewrite generic AI-sounding prose. These are not absolute bans; they are high-suspicion patterns.

## 1. Throat-clearing and didactic signposting

Flag openings like:
- "It is important to note that..."
- "It is worth considering that..."
- "Certainly, here is..."
- "In today's rapidly evolving..."
- "Let's dive in"
- "Let's break this down"
- "When it comes to..."

**Why it reads as AI:** It delays the point and performs helpfulness instead of delivering substance.

**Default fix:** Delete the setup and start with the actual claim, example, or question.

## 2. Inflated vocabulary

Strongly question words like:
- delve
- leverage
- robust
- comprehensive
- seamless
- pivotal
- transformative
- groundbreaking
- innovative
- holistic
- nuanced (when used as empty praise)
- underscore
- foster
- facilitate
- landscape
- realm
- navigate (figurative)

**Why it reads as AI:** These words are statistically common in generic assistant prose and business fluff.

**Default fix:** Replace with plain, direct verbs and concrete nouns.

## 3. False-insight structures

Flag structures like:
- "It's not just X, it's Y"
- "This isn't about X. It's about Y."
- "Not only X, but also Y"
- "No X. No Y. Just Z."
- "One of the most important..."

**Why it reads as AI:** These mimic emphasis and insight without necessarily adding information.

**Default fix:** State the actual point directly.

## 4. Generic authority without proof

Flag claims like:
- "Many companies..."
- "Experts say..."
- "Studies show..."
- "Users value..."
- "This significantly improves..."
- "Leading brands..."

**Why it reads as AI:** The prose gestures at authority while avoiding specifics.

**Default fix:** Add names, examples, numbers, or explicit uncertainty. If you cannot support the claim, narrow or remove it.

## 5. Uniform rhythm

Look for:
- multiple sentences in a row with similar length
- paragraphs with the same cadence throughout
- repeated "topic sentence -> explanation -> example -> bridge" structure

**Why it reads as AI:** AI defaults to smooth, statistically even cadence.

**Default fix:** Mix short and long sentences. Use fragments sparingly. Vary paragraph shapes.

## 6. Over-formatting and over-signposting

Flag patterns like:
- excessive headers or bullets for simple points
- two-item bullet lists that should be one sentence
- repetitive list formatting such as "Bold term: explanation"
- transitions like "first," "second," "finally" when not needed

**Why it reads as AI:** The structure becomes visibly templated.

**Default fix:** Collapse weak lists, reduce scaffolding, and let the prose carry more of the meaning.

## 7. Hype without evidence

Flag adjectives like:
- revolutionary
- game-changing
- unparalleled
- cutting-edge
- best-in-class
- world-class

**Why it reads as AI:** They overclaim while saying little.

**Default fix:** Replace with a specific result, comparison, or observable fact. If none exists, delete the hype.

## 8. Empty abstraction

Flag vague nouns like:
- solution
- ecosystem
- strategy
- capability
- framework
- innovation
- value
- efficiency
- impact

**Why it reads as AI:** Abstract nouns let the sentence sound substantial without becoming concrete.

**Default fix:** Name the actual object, team, behavior, metric, or decision.

## 9. Overuse of em dashes or parenthetical cleverness

Look for:
- multiple em dashes in a short piece
- repeated side-comments that feel manufactured

**Why it reads as AI:** Models often overuse em dashes to simulate rhythm and sophistication.

**Default fix:** Use commas, periods, or one cleaner aside. Keep the rhythm varied without leaning on the same trick.

## 10. Generic conclusions

Flag endings like:
- "In conclusion..."
- "As the landscape continues to evolve..."
- "By implementing these strategies..."
- "Ultimately, success depends on..."

**Why it reads as AI:** The ending summarizes rather than lands.

**Default fix:** End with a concrete point, clear recommendation, sharp observation, or real question.

## 11. Humanizing principles

When revising, prefer:
- direct claims over padded setup
- examples over abstractions
- named entities over anonymous groups
- lived experience over vague authority
- rhythm over monotony
- a real point of view over synthetic balance

## 12. Do not over-correct

Avoid turning every draft into the same faux-human style.

Do not:
- inject fake personality
- invent anecdotes
- add slang that does not fit the audience
- force short punchy sentences everywhere
- remove all polish when the context calls for professionalism

The goal is not "casual." The goal is "human, specific, and alive."
