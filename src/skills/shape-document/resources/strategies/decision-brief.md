# Decision Brief Strategy

Use for a short approval or review artifact whose reader needs to understand one decision, why it is supported, what remains open, and what action to take.

## Fit And Inputs

Use only when the composition names one primary reader action and has a selected decision or explicit unresolved decision. Do not use it to hide multi-option design work, an unresolved material conflict, or a broad explanatory goal.

Carry forward the composition's pinned snapshots, selected-entry manifest, omissions, and blocking gaps. A decision brief cannot convert an observation into an approved direction.

## Reader Journey

1. Put the decision or ask first, in a short `Decision` section.
2. State scope and what is deliberately out of scope.
3. Show only the evidence and trade-offs needed to evaluate that decision.
4. Make operational risk, uncertainty, and follow-up visible before the final action.
5. End with a concrete `Requested action` and owner or approval boundary when supplied by the Case.

Use a compact comparison table only when it preserves the authority, confidence, and status of all material claims. Label historical, rejected, or deferred material in prose or in the table itself. Do not make visual grouping imply priority, approval, causality, or completeness not present in the Case.

## Anchors And Disclosure

Give every material decision, evidence claim, risk, gap, and table row a stable anchor such as `DECISION`, `EVIDENCE-CAPACITY`, `RISK-LATENCY`, or `OPTIONS`. An anchor may be a heading ID or an explicit HTML comment in Markdown. Trace semantic units to anchors, not to incidental paragraph positions.

Keep these visible in the main reader flow: the decision status, material caveat, primary risk, and requested action. Supporting historical detail may be deferred only when the selection manifest accounts for it.

## Quality Bar

The reader should be able to answer without opening sources: what is being decided, what evidence supports it, what alternative was rejected or deferred, which caveat matters, and what they must do next. If the answer requires source interpretation, return to composition or reconciliation rather than adding unsupported prose.
