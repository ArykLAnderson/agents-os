# Mental Model Strategy

Use for explanation documents whose reader must understand concepts and relationships before acting on a conclusion.

## Reader Journey

1. Name the practical problem and bounded reader action.
2. Ground the smallest set of concepts needed for the reader to form the system boundary; do not turn the grounding into a glossary march.
3. Move between model, consequence, and example as needed. A concept may be introduced through a concrete consequence and named afterward when that produces more natural understanding.
4. Explain lifecycle and relationships with a textual path before or alongside a visual.
5. Use one representative example without claiming it is exhaustive, and let it recur where it clarifies different relationships rather than confining it to one checklist section.
6. State the safety boundary, proof boundary, and what the model cannot establish.
7. End with the reader action or a handoff to a review briefing.

## Anchors And Fit

Use stable semantic anchors appropriate to the actual argument; do not require visible sections named `problem`, `vocabulary`, `lifecycle`, `relationships`, `example`, `boundaries`, or `next-action`. Define a concept before depending on its shorthand, but allow its grounding, example, and consequence to be woven through the reader journey. Label examples and diagrams as bounded representations.

This strategy does not fit when the reader already understands the system and only needs a narrowly scoped approval or review ask; use `review-briefing` then. It must not replace a Case decision, source trace, or approval record.
